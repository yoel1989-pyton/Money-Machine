[ACT 1: THE HOOK]

[NARRATOR]

I discovered something unsettling. The middle class, the backbone of our society, is vanishing—not by accident, but by design.

[VISUAL: Slow-motion footage of empty suburban streets, closed storefronts, and foreclosed homes]

Behind closed doors, a system is at play, engineered to erode the very foundation of economic stability for millions. This isn't a conspiracy theory; it's a stark reality.

Consider this: since the late 1970s, the share of income held by the middle 60% of Americans has plummeted from 53% to 45%. Meanwhile, the richest 1% have seen their share nearly double. It's not just numbers—it's a deliberate shift, crafted by those who profit from our ignorance.

[VISUAL: Graphs showing income distribution changes over decades]

As we peel back the layers, you'll see what's been hidden from view. The question is, why? And more importantly, why should you care?

[ACT 2: THE MECHANISM]

[NARRATOR]

The mechanism behind the disappearance of the middle class is as complex as it is insidious. It starts with stagnant wages—since 1979, productivity has increased by 69.6%, while hourly pay has only grown by 11.6%.

[VISUAL: Factory workers and office employees at their desks, numbers overlaying screen]

Corporations, once pillars of community growth, have shifted focus. Tax policies changed, favoring capital over labor. In 1980, the top corporate tax rate was 46%; today, it's 21%. This shift incentivizes stock buybacks over wage increases, lining the pockets of executives and shareholders.

[VISUAL: Stock market tickers, corporate boardrooms]

And let's not overlook the financial sector. Banks, deregulated in the late 20th century, found new ways to exploit the system. Subprime mortgages, payday loans, and credit card debts became traps for the unwary. The average American household owes over $137,000 in debt, including mortgages, car loans, and credit cards.

[VISUAL: A family sorting through bills, calculator in hand]

Education, once a ladder to upward mobility, now shackles graduates with an average debt of $37,000, limiting their economic freedom. These are not isolated issues; they're interconnected parts of a system that siphons wealth upwards.

The system is designed to be opaque, hidden behind layers of financial jargon and policies that go unnoticed by the many—yet benefit the few.

[ACT 3: THE PLAYERS]

[NARRATOR]

Who are the masterminds behind this system? To unravel this, we must look at the architects of policy and finance.

The Reagan administration's embrace of supply-side economics marked a pivotal shift. Tax cuts for the wealthy, deregulation, and a dismantling of union power were not just political choices; they were strategic moves that laid the groundwork for today's landscape.

[VISUAL: Archival footage of Ronald Reagan giving speeches]

Institutions like the Federal Reserve, with its focus on controlling inflation at the expense of employment, play their part. Decisions made by figures like Alan Greenspan prioritized financial markets over working-class stability.

[VISUAL: Federal Reserve building, Alan Greenspan speaking]

On Wall Street, giants like Goldman Sachs and JPMorgan Chase thrive on volatility and debt. They influence policy through lobbying—a staggering $2.6 billion spent on financial lobbying since 1998.

[VISUAL: Wall Street, lobbyists shaking hands with politicians]

And then there's the tech sector, where companies like Amazon and Google capitalize on tax loopholes, paying little to no taxes while their market dominance crushes small businesses.

These players operate with impunity, benefitting from a system they helped design, a system that rewards wealth accumulation over equitable distribution.

[ACT 4: THE CONSEQUENCES]

[NARRATOR]

Why should this matter to you? Because the erosion of the middle class isn't just an economic issue—it's a societal one.

If trends continue, by 2030, the middle class could shrink to under 50% of the population, a tipping point that threatens social cohesion.

[VISUAL: Families struggling with bills, empty shopping malls]

With reduced purchasing power, consumer demand falls, leading to slower economic growth and fewer job opportunities. The dream of homeownership, once attainable, becomes a distant fantasy for many.

[VISUAL: For sale signs in front of houses, families living in rented apartments]

A society polarized between the very rich and the very poor breeds instability. History warns us: the fall of Rome, the French Revolution, echoes of what happens when inequality spirals out of control.

[VISUAL: Historical footage of protests, revolutions]

And let's not forget democracy. As wealth concentrates, so does political power. Policies increasingly reflect the interests of the elite, leaving the majority disenfranchised.

[VISUAL: Political rallies, voting lines]

This isn't just a future problem—it's our present reality, one that demands urgent attention.

[NARRATOR]

Understand the policies shaping our world: tax reforms, labor laws, and financial regulations. Vote for representatives who prioritize economic equality and hold them accountable.

Support local businesses and demand corporate responsibility. Push for transparency in lobbying and campaign financing.

[VISUAL: Small business storefronts, protestors outside corporate buildings]
